How to Clear Brambles Properly Without Them Coming Back

Brambles are the gardeners' enemy. They grow fast, tangle into impenetrable thickets, and regrow from any root left in the ground. Cutting them down is easy. Stopping them coming back is harder. This guide explains how to clear brambles properly so they do not return.

The short answer

Quick Answer

To clear brambles properly: cut the top growth to ground level, dig out every root crown with a mattock, rake up all debris, and monitor for regrowth. Treat any regrowth with a glyphosate-based weedkiller in late summer when the plants are sending energy to the roots. On chalky Chiltern soils, bramble roots are easier to dig out than on heavy clay in the vale around Aylesbury, where the root crowns sit in sticky, wet soil.

Why Brambles Are So Persistent

Brambles (Rubus fruticosus) are native UK plants with a survival strategy that makes them hard to kill. They spread by seed (bird-dropped), by tip-rooting (the long stems root where they touch the ground), and by suckering from the root crown. A single missed root crown can produce a new thicket in a single growing season. Cutting the top growth without digging out the root is a waste of time, the plant will simply regrow from the base.

In the Chilterns, brambles thrive along woodland edges, in neglected corners, and on the banks of fields and gardens. They are particularly aggressive on the edges of gardens near beech woods around High Wycombe, Marlow and Henley-on-Thames, where birds drop seeds and the semi-shade conditions suit them perfectly.

Step 1: Cut the Top Growth

Use a brush cutter, secateurs or loppers to cut all the top growth to ground level. Wear thick gloves, a long-sleeved top and eye protection, the thorns are vicious and can cause nasty scratches that get infected. Cut the stems into manageable lengths and pile them up for removal. A 10 square metre bramble thicket will produce 4 to 6 bin bags of cut material.

If the brambles are tangled with fences or trees, work carefully to avoid damaging the underlying structure. Pull the cut stems out a handful at a time, rather than yanking whole armfuls which can pull fence panels with them.

Step 2: Dig Out the Root Crowns

This is the most important step and the one most people skip. Brambles regrow from the root crown, the woody swelling where the stems meet the roots. Use a mattock to dig out every crown. Go 15 to 20cm deep to get the whole crown. Any fragment left will regrow.

On chalky soils in the Chilterns, the root crowns sit in relatively free-draining soil and come out cleanly with a mattock. On heavy clay in the Vale of Aylesbury or around Didcot, the soil sticks to the roots and you may need to use a spade as well as a mattock. Either way, the goal is to remove the entire woody crown, not just chop through it.

Push the mattock into the soil about 15cm from the crown, lever it back, and lift the crown out. Check the hole for any broken-off root fragments. These will regrow if left.

Step 3: Rake Up All Debris

Rake up all the cut stems, leaves and root fragments. Bramble stems can root where they touch the ground, so do not leave any lying around. Burn the waste if you have a burn permit, take it to the tip, or have it removed by us as part of a clearance (see our clearance service). We take all waste away.

Step 4: Monitor for Regrowth

Check the area every 2 weeks for the first 3 months. Any root fragment you missed will produce a new shoot within 4 to 6 weeks in the growing season. Dig out any regrowth immediately, do not let it get established. A small shoot is easy to dig out, a 6-month-old thicket is not.

Step 5: Treat Stubborn Regrowth

If regrowth persists after 2 or 3 digs, treat with a glyphosate-based weedkiller in late summer (August to September). At this time of year, brambles are sending energy down to the roots for winter, so the weedkiller is carried down to kill the root system. Spray on a dry, calm day when no rain is forecast for 6 hours. Follow the label instructions carefully.

Note: glyphosate is the only weedkiller that genuinely works on brambles. Contact weedkillers burn the leaves but do not kill the root. Persistent organic gardeners can keep digging out regrowth for 2 to 3 seasons instead, which eventually exhausts the root system.

When to Clear Brambles

Winter (November to February) is the best time. The brambles have died back, making them easier to cut and lighter to carry. The lack of leaves also means you can see the root crowns more clearly. The ground is softer after winter rain, which makes digging easier. Avoid clearing brambles during bird nesting season (March to August) if the thicket is being used by nesting birds, as it is an offence under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 to damage or destroy an active nest.

Tools You Need

  • Brush cutter or secateurs and loppers
  • Mattock for digging out root crowns
  • Spade for heavy clay soils
  • Thick gloves, long-sleeved top, and eye protection
  • Wheelbarrow for waste
  • Rake
  • Glyphosate weedkiller and sprayer (for stubborn regrowth only)

Cost of Bramble Clearance

Bramble clearance is priced by area and density. Light bramble (mostly top growth, easy roots) costs 8 to 12 per square metre. Heavy bramble (mature thicket, root crowns established) costs 15 to 25 per square metre. A typical 20 square metre patch costs 200 to 500 cleared and waste removed. Common Mistakes

  • Cutting without digging. Just cutting the top growth leaves the root crown in the ground. The bramble will be back within weeks.
  • Leaving cut stems on the ground. Bramble stems root where they touch soil. Always remove every piece.
  • Using contact weedkiller. Contact weedkillers burn the leaves but do not kill the root. Use glyphosate, or just dig.
  • Not wearing protection. Bramble thorns cause nasty scratches that can get infected. Always wear thick gloves and long sleeves.
  • Clearing in nesting season without checking. Bramble thickets are popular nest sites. Always check carefully before clearing between March and August.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will brambles come back if I just cut them?

Yes. Cutting removes the top growth but leaves the root crown, which will regrow within weeks. You must dig out the root crown to permanently remove brambles.

Is there a weedkiller that kills brambles but not grass?

Glyphosate will kill any plant it touches, including grass. There is no selective weedkiller for brambles in lawns. The only options are to dig them out, or to carefully paint glyphosate onto the bramble leaves without touching the grass.

How long until I can replant the area?

Immediately after digging out the root crowns. If you have used glyphosate, wait 4 weeks for it to break down before replanting. See our soil preparation guide for what to do next.
