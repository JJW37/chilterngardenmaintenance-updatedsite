Mahonia

Mahonia aquifolium and Mahonia x media
Evergreen shrub Berberidaceae

Mahonia is the evergreen shrub that flowers in the depths of winter, throwing up spikes of yellow scented flowers from November to March when almost nothing else is in bloom. The prickly evergreen leaves give year round structure and the blue-black berries that follow feed blackbirds through spring. In a Chiltern garden it is a plant for the back of a shady border or against a north wall where little else will thrive, and it asks for very little in return.

Light
Shade or partial shade. Will tolerate sun in cool gardens
Temperature
Hardy to about -15C
Water
Drought tolerant once established. Water weekly in the first summer
Mature size
1.5 to 3m tall depending on species. M. aquifolium is lower, around 60cm
Soil type
Any reasonable soil including chalk and clay
Soil pH
pH 5.0 to 7.5
Drainage
Tolerates most soils including dry shade
Feeding
Light feed in spring. A mulch of compost is plenty
The short answer

Quick Answer

Mahonia thrives in partial shade or full shade on chalky, clay soils (pH 5-7.5), well-drained soil. Reaches 1.5 to 3m tall depending on species. m. aquifolium is lower, around 60cm. Excellent chalk tolerance. Hardy to around -15°C.

Pruning

When: After flowering in early spring

How: Shorten tall stems by a third to encourage branching. Remove old bare stems at the base

Origin, history and folklore

Origin: Asia and North America depending on species

Native or exotic: Exotic. M. aquifolium (Oregon grape) is from western North America

History: M. aquifolium was introduced to UK gardens in 1823. M. x media hybrids were raised in Irish gardens in the 1950s and 1960s, including the popular Charity and Winter Sun

Folklore: The berries of Oregon grape were used by Native American tribes of the Pacific Northwest as a dye and food source

Fun fact: Mahonia is named after Bernard McMahon, an Irish born American horticulturist who published the first seed catalogue in the United States in 1804

Historical medicinal uses: Oregon grape root contains berberine and has been used in traditional medicine for skin conditions. Not for home use without qualified advice

Wildlife and biodiversity

Pollinators: Outstanding winter nectar for bumblebees on warm days when nothing else is flowering [4][5]

Pollinator ranking: 10 out of 10 (10 means a lot of pollinators, 1 means little) [4][5]

Biodiversity value: 8 out of 10 High. Winter nectar when almost nothing else flowers. Berries for blackbirds in early spring[4][5][13]

Seasonal highlight: 7 out of 10 Yellow scented flower spikes from November to March, followed by blue-black berries[4]

Companion planting and pests

Companion planting: Combine with hellebores and ferns in a shady winter border

Pest control: Spiny leaves deter rabbits and deer

Common diseases and issues: Rust, powdery mildew. See RHS powdery mildew advice

Invasive note: Not invasive. Self seeds mildly in warm gardens

CGM care note

Mahonia gets leggy with age, with bare stems and a tuft of leaves on top. We rejuvenate these by cutting the tallest stems back to 60cm in March after flowering. The plant regrows from the cut, and within two seasons it is a full bushy shape again.

Plant relationships, Mahonia

How Mahonia fits with other plants in a real Chiltern garden, what it works well with, what to avoid, and what to use instead in tricky spots.

Works well with

  • Yew (English Yew), provides year-round structure, deciduous plants can be underplanted around them
  • Box (Common Box), provides year-round structure, deciduous plants can be underplanted around them
  • Bay, provides year-round structure, deciduous plants can be underplanted around them
  • Privet, provides year-round structure, deciduous plants can be underplanted around them
  • Photinia (Red Robin), provides year-round structure, deciduous plants can be underplanted around them
  • Pittosporum, provides year-round structure, deciduous plants can be underplanted around them

Avoid planting beside

  • Delphinium, different soil or light needs
  • Hakonechloa (Japanese Forest Grass), different soil or light needs

Similar alternatives

  • Roses
  • Hydrangea
  • Peony
  • Philadelphus (Mock Orange)

Better choice for exposed sites

  • Beech
  • Blackthorn
  • Cotoneaster

Better choice for shade

  • Bugle
  • Amelanchier (Serviceberry, Juneberry)
  • Anemones (Woodland and Balkan)

Replacement for failed box

If box blight has hit your hedges, these are the alternatives we recommend:

  • Yew (English Yew)
  • Privet
  • Euonymus (Spindle)
  • Pittosporum

Seasonal succession partners

Plants with non-overlapping flowering windows to extend the season:

  • Agapanthus (African Lily)
  • Bugle
  • Alliums (Ornamental Onions)
  • Amelanchier (Serviceberry, Juneberry)

Chiltern growing year

12-month care calendar

Use this as a seasonal prompt for this plant. Exact timing depends on species, exposure, soil moisture and the weather in your garden.

JanuaryCheck winter damage and protect vulnerable new growth.
FebruaryPrune only where the plant and weather allow; plan spring work.
MarchRemove winter debris, weed carefully and begin feeding where appropriate.
AprilPlant in workable soil and water new plants during dry spells.
MayMulch before the ground dries and watch for pests and new disease.
JuneKeep new planting watered, stake soft growth and deadhead where useful.
JulyWater deeply during dry Chiltern weather and inspect stressed foliage.
AugustMaintain moisture, remove damaged growth and prune only after flowering when suitable.
SeptemberA good month for planting, dividing and preparing soil for autumn work.
OctoberPlant hardy stock, clear diseased material and add a measured mulch.
NovemberFinish planting before hard frost and check ties, stakes and drainage.
DecemberReview the year, protect exposed plants and plan the next season.
