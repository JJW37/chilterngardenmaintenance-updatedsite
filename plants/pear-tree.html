Pear Tree

Pyrus communis
Deciduous fruit tree Rosaceae

Pears are an elegant tree with beautiful spring blossom and a long season of fruit. They prefer a slightly warmer and more sheltered spot than apples and crop best in southern England, so the Chilterns is well suited. Conference and Concorde are reliable self-fertile varieties that work for most gardens. Pears store well and ripen indoors after picking. Trained forms such as cordons and espaliers suit small gardens well.

Light
Full sun, sheltered position
Temperature
Hardy to about -20C. Blossom is frost sensitive
Water
Regular in first season. Deep watering in dry spells
Mature size
3 to 5m depending on rootstock
Soil type
Any reasonable soil. Prefers slightly heavier soils
Soil pH
pH 6.0 to 7.0
Drainage
Well drained
Feeding
Balanced fertiliser in February, mulch in spring
The short answer

Quick Answer

Pear Tree thrives in full sun on most soils (pH 6-7), free-draining. Reaches 3 to 5m depending on rootstock. Hardy to around -20°C.

Pruning

When: November to February for established trees. Trained forms: summer

How: Open goblet shape. Pears fruit mostly on spurs so be careful not to over-prune. See RHS pear pruning guide

Origin, history and folklore

Origin: Europe and western Asia

Native or exotic: Exotic. Wild pear (Pyrus pyraster) is native but very rare in the UK

History: Cultivated in Europe for at least 3,000 years. Romans grew pears extensively

Folklore: In ancient Greece, pears were sacred to Hera, queen of the gods

Fun fact: The UK National Fruit Collection at Brogdale holds over 500 pear varieties

Historical medicinal uses: Pears are high in fibre and low in allergens, often recommended as a first fruit for weaning babies

Wildlife and biodiversity

Pollinators: Bees visit blossom. Most varieties need a pollination partner [3][4][5]

Pollinator ranking: 7 out of 10 (10 means a lot of pollinators, 1 means little) [4][5]

Biodiversity value: Medium to high. Blossom and fruit support wildlife [3][4][5][13]

Seasonal highlight: White blossom in April. Fruit August to October [3][4]

Companion planting and pests

Companion planting: Underplant with chives, alliums or comfrey to attract pollinators and suppress grass

Pest control: Grease bands for winter moth, pheromone traps for codling moth

Common diseases and issues: Pear scab, canker, fireblight, pear rust. See RHS pear problems advice

Invasive note: Not invasive

CGM care note

For small gardens we use Quince C rootstock which gives a tree of about 2.5 to 3m. Quince A gives a slightly larger tree. Pears fruit best on horizontal branches, so we train them as cordons or espaliers against a fence when space allows.
