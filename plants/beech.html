Beech

Fagus sylvatica
Deciduous tree, used as hedging Fagaceae

Beech is one of the finest hedging plants for the UK, holding its russet leaves through winter and flushing bright green in May. On the chalky soils of the Chilterns, where beech woodland is the native tree cover, it is the natural choice for a hedge or a single specimen. It makes a hedge that is dense, long lived and quietly beautiful, and it clips as crisply as yew if you have the patience. For a formal boundary in a Chiltern garden there is nothing better.

Light
Sun or light shade. Best in full sun for hedging
Temperature
Hardy to about -25C
Water
Drought tolerant once established. Water weekly in the first season
Mature size
1.5 to 4m as a hedge, 20m plus as a tree
Soil type
Prefers chalky or free draining loam. Dislikes heavy clay
Soil pH
pH 5.5 to 8.0, thrives on alkaline chalk
Drainage
Dislikes wet ground. Will not tolerate sitting water in winter
Feeding
Light feed in spring for young plants. A mulch of compost helps establishment
The short answer

Quick Answer

Beech thrives in full sun or full shade on chalky, loamy, clay soils (pH 5.5-8), well-drained soil. Reaches 1.5 to 4m as a hedge, 20m plus as a tree. Excellent chalk tolerance. Hardy to around -25°C.

Pruning

When: Late August for the main prune. Light trim in February if needed

How: Shear to shape. Beech will regrow from old wood if cut in late winter

Origin, history and folklore

Origin: Southern England and across Europe

Native or exotic: Native. The Chilterns beechwoods are some of the finest in Britain

History: Beech woodland expanded in southern England in the medieval period. The Chilterns beechwoods supplied chair legs and furniture for High Wycombe's furniture trade from the 1800s onwards

Folklore: Beech was associated with learning and written knowledge in medieval Europe. Carvings were made on the smooth grey bark

Fun fact: Beech trees can live for 300 years. The Latin name Fagus is the root of the English word book, as early writing tablets were made of beech

Historical medicinal uses: None in modern use. Young leaves were traditionally used in salads and have a mild lemon flavour

Wildlife and biodiversity

Pollinators: Low. Wind pollinated. Beech honeydew supports insects in summer [4][5]

Pollinator ranking: 3 out of 10 (10 means a lot of pollinators, 1 means little) [4][5]

Biodiversity value: High. Supports many moth species and fungi. Tawny owls nest in old trees [3][4][5][13]

Seasonal highlight: Fresh lime green foliage in May and copper leaves held through winter on clipped hedges [4]

Companion planting and pests

Companion planting: Underplant with hellebores, wood anemones and bluebells in light shade

Pest control: Generally trouble free

Common diseases and issues: Beech bark disease, aphids, honey fungus. See RHS honey fungus advice

Invasive note: Not invasive. Will self seed in nearby woodland edge

CGM care note

For a new beech hedge we plant at 30cm spacing in a double staggered row, mulch heavily, and prune the leading shoot by a third in the first winter. The lower laterals fill in within two seasons and you have a proper hedge by year three.
