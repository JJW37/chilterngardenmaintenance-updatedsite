Bergenia (Elephant Ears)

Bergenia cordifolia and hybrids
Evergreen perennial Saxifragaceae

Bergenia is a tough, no-nonsense plant for difficult sites. The huge rounded leathery leaves give it the common name elephant ears, and in winter they turn rich shades of red and bronze. Pink or white flowers appear on stout stems in early spring, making bergenias one of the first perennials to bloom. They are unfussy about soil and tolerate dry shade surprisingly well.

Light
Full sun to full shade. Best leaf colour in sun
Temperature
Hardy to about -20C
Water
Tolerates drought once established. Prefers moist soil
Mature size
40 to 60cm tall and wide
Soil type
Any reasonable soil including chalk
Soil pH
pH 6.0 to 7.5
Drainage
Well drained. Tolerates clay
Feeding
Spring mulch and light feed
The short answer

Quick Answer

Bergenia (Elephant Ears) thrives in full sun or full shade on chalky soils (pH 6-7.5), free-draining. Reaches 40 to 60cm tall and wide. Excellent chalk tolerance. Hardy to around -20°C.

Pruning

When: Tidy dead leaves in spring after flowering

How: Pull off tatty old leaves at the base. Deadhead flower stems to ground level

Origin, history and folklore

Origin: Central Asia, including Siberia and the Himalayas

Native or exotic: Exotic

History: Introduced to UK gardens in 1819. Named after German botanist Karl August von Bergen

Folklore: In Russia, bergenia was called 'badan' and the dead leaves were used to make a tea drunk for stamina on long journeys

Fun fact: Bergenia leaves are so thick and leathery that they were used historically as sandpaper by gardeners cleaning tools

Historical medicinal uses: Used in Siberian folk medicine for wounds. Not for self-medication

Wildlife and biodiversity

Pollinators: Early nectar for emerging bumblebees [4][5]

Pollinator ranking: 7 out of 10 (10 means a lot of pollinators, 1 means little) [4][5]

Biodiversity value: Medium. Evergreen leaves shelter overwintering insects [4][5][13]

Seasonal highlight: Pink flowers March to April. Leaves turn red-bronze in cold winter weather [4]

Companion planting and pests

Companion planting: Pairs with hellebores, snowdrops and pulmonaria in winter borders

Pest control: Generally pest free. Vine weevil adults chew notches in leaf edges but rarely damage the plant seriously

Common diseases and issues: Leaf spot and brown marks on old leaves. See RHS Bergenia page

Invasive note: Not invasive. Slowly spreads by rhizomes

CGM care note

We use bergenia as ground cover in dry shade under trees where little else grows. The winter leaf colour is better on poorer soil, so we never feed them heavily.
