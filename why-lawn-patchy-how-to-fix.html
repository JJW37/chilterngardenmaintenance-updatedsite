Why Your Lawn Is Patchy and How to Fix It

A patchy lawn is one of the most common complaints we hear from garden owners across Oxfordshire, Buckinghamshire, Berkshire and Bedfordshire. Patches can be caused by anything from dog urine to shade to disease. The fix depends on the cause. This guide walks through the most common causes of patchy lawns in the Chilterns and how to fix each one.

The short answer

Quick Answer

Patchy lawns are caused by shade, compaction, dog damage, drought, disease, pests, poor mowing or poor feeding. Identify the cause first, then fix it. Most patches can be repaired by overseeding with the right seed mix. For widespread patches, a full lawn renovation may be needed.

Common Causes of Patchy Lawns in the Chilterns

1. Shade

The most common cause in our area. Grass needs at least 4 hours of direct sun. Under trees, along north-facing walls and beside fences in gardens around Beaconsfield, Gerrards Cross and Great Missenden, grass thins and patches develop. The mature trees that make Chiltern gardens so attractive are also the main cause of shade-related lawn patches.

Fix: Prune trees to let in light, or overseed with a shade-tolerant fescue mix. See our lawn planning tool for seed recommendations. In heavily shaded areas, consider replacing the lawn with shade-tolerant ground cover.

2. Compaction

Heavy foot traffic, children playing and heavy mowing compact the soil. Grass roots cannot penetrate compacted soil, so the grass thins. This is particularly common on clay soils in Aylesbury, Bicester and Didcot, where the soil compacts easily when wet.

Fix: Aerate annually with a hollow-tine aerator. See our aeration guide.

3. Dog damage

Dog urine contains nitrogen that burns the grass, creating circular patches. Female dogs cause more damage because they squat rather than lift. This is a common problem in family gardens across Oxfordshire and Buckinghamshire.

Fix: Water the area immediately after the dog urinates to dilute the nitrogen. For existing patches, rake out the dead grass, add soil and overseed with a hard-wearing ryegrass mix.

4. Drought

In dry summers, grass goes dormant and patches appear. This is natural and the grass usually recovers when rain returns. However, on the free-draining chalk soils of the Chilterns around High Wycombe, Stokenchurch and Watlington, drought patches can become permanent if the dry spell is long enough.

Fix: Water deeply once a week during drought. See our dry summer lawn guide. Overseed drought-damaged areas in autumn with a drought-tolerant fescue mix.

5. Disease

Fungal diseases like red thread, fairy rings and snow mould cause patches. These are more common in wet conditions and on lawns that are over-fed with nitrogen. Lawns in the Thames Valley around Reading and Twyford can be particularly vulnerable to fungal diseases in wet autumns.

Fix: Improve drainage, reduce thatch, and apply a fungicide if needed. See RHS lawn disease guidance.

6. Pests

Leatherjackets (crane fly larvae) and chafer grubs eat grass roots, causing patches. Birds pecking at the soil for the grubs make it worse. This is an increasing problem across the Chilterns and Thames Valley.

Fix: Apply a biological nematode treatment in autumn. See RHS leatherjacket guidance.

7. Poor mowing

Cutting too short scalps the grass and weakens it. This is a common problem when homeowners cut the lawn themselves and set the mower too low, especially during the hot Chiltern summers when the grass is already stressed.

Fix: Never cut more than one third of the grass height in one mowing. Set the mower to 30 to 40mm for family lawns, higher in dry weather.

8. Poor feeding

Under-fed grass is weak and patchy. On chalky Chiltern soils, nutrients are easily leached out, so regular feeding is even more important than on heavier soils.

Fix: Apply a balanced fertiliser in spring and a low-nitrogen feed in autumn.

How to Repair Patches

  1. Rake out the dead grass with a spring-tine rake
  2. Loosen the soil surface with a garden fork
  3. Add a thin layer of topsoil or compost
  4. Sow seed at 30g per square metre
  5. Rake in lightly and water
  6. Keep moist for 3 weeks
  7. Protect from birds with netting

Frequently Asked Questions

How do I know if my patches are caused by pests?

Lift a section of turf. If you see white grubs or grey-brown larvae in the soil, you have chafer grubs or leatherjackets. Birds pecking at the lawn is also a sign.

Can I repair patches in winter?

No. Grass seed will not germinate in cold soil. Wait until March or April when the soil warms up.

How long do patches take to fill in?

With the right seed and regular watering, patches fill in within 3 to 6 weeks in spring or autumn.

Estate Note

On mature lawns, check for leatherjackets and chafer grubs before scarifying. Established turf recovers more slowly than newly seeded areas, so adjust the renovation intensity accordingly.
