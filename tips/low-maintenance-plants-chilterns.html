Best Low-Maintenance Plants for Chiltern Gardens

Chiltern gardens sit on chalk, with free-draining alkaline soil that suits some plants perfectly and defeats others. The trick to a low-maintenance garden here is choosing plants that thrive in these conditions rather than fighting them. This guide lists ten plants we install again and again for clients who want a beautiful garden without constant work.

Why Chiltern Soil Is Different

The Chiltern Hills run from Oxfordshire across Buckinghamshire to Hertfordshire. The underlying rock is chalk, which gives a shallow, free-draining, alkaline soil. In dry summers the soil bakes hard. In wet winters it can waterlog briefly but drains quickly.

This is bad news for acid-loving plants like rhododendrons and camellias, which need to be grown in pots of ericaceous compost. But it is excellent news for Mediterranean plants, silver foliage, herbs and many classic English cottage garden plants.

The plants in this list all thrive in Chiltern conditions with very little attention once established.

1. Lavender

The classic Chiltern plant. Lavender loves the free-draining chalky soil and full sun. Plant in spring, water through the first summer, then leave alone. Cut back by one third in late August after flowering. A single plant will flower for 6 to 8 weeks and attract dozens of bees.

Full lavender profile

2. Rosemary

Another Mediterranean native that loves Chiltern conditions. Rosemary is evergreen, drought tolerant, and flowers in late winter when bees need it most. Plant in full sun. Prune lightly after flowering. Choose an upright variety like Miss Jessopp's Upright for a formal hedge, or a prostrate form for trailing over walls.

Full rosemary profile

3. Salvia (Perennial Sage)

Hardy salvias flower from May to October if sheared back after the first flush. They love sun and well-drained soil. The varieties Mainacht, Caradonna and Amethyst are reliable and long flowering. Salvias are also aromatic, which deters rabbits and deer.

Full salvia profile

4. Hardy Geranium (Cranesbill)

The most useful ground cover plant in any garden. Hardy geraniums flower for months, suppress weeds, and tolerate almost any condition. The variety Rozanne flowers from May to October with no deadheading. Plant at the front of borders in drifts of 3 or 5.

Full geranium profile

5. Nepeta (Catmint)

Catmint is the perfect partner for roses. It flowers in late spring and again in late summer, with soft violet-blue flowers and grey-green foliage. The variety Walker's Low is the most reliable. Bees love it. Cut back hard after the first flush for a tidy second show.

Full nepeta profile

6. Verbena bonariensis

The see-through plant for the back of a border. Tall wiry stems carry clusters of tiny purple flowers from July to October. Butterflies adore it. Self-seeds gently in warm gardens. Plant in full sun in well-drained soil.

Full verbena profile

7. Alliums

Ornamental onions flower in late May and June, bridging the gap between spring bulbs and summer perennials. The big round flower heads of Purple Sensation and Globemaster are dramatic and the seed heads stay decorative for months. Plant bulbs in October in clusters of 7 or 9.

Full allium profile

8. Stipa tenuissima

A low tousled grass that catches the light beautifully. Stipa needs full sun and free-draining soil. Once established it needs nothing except a comb through with gloved hands in spring to remove dead leaves. Pairs beautifully with lavender and salvias.

Full stipa profile

9. Yew

For structure and hedging, yew is hard to beat. Native to the UK, it grows in any reasonable soil including chalk. Yew clips beautifully into formal hedges and topiary, and regenerates from old wood. Plant a yew hedge once and it will still be there in 100 years.

Full yew profile

10. Hellebores

For shadier parts of the garden, hellebores are the answer. They flower from January to April when little else is happening. The new hybrid varieties have upward-facing flowers in pink, white, purple and almost black. Cut the old leaves off in January to show off the flowers.

Full hellebore profile

Plants to Avoid on Chiltern Soil

Some popular plants struggle on chalky soil and are best avoided unless you are willing to grow them in pots:

  • Rhododendrons: need acid soil, will go yellow and die on chalk
  • Camellias: same as rhododendrons
  • Blueberries: need acid soil
  • Pieris: needs acid soil
  • Heathers (calluna and erica): most need acid soil. Some erica varieties tolerate chalk.
  • Magnolias: many but not all need acid soil. Magnolia kobus and stellata tolerate chalk.

If you really want these plants, grow them in large pots of ericaceous compost and feed with an ericaceous plant food twice a year.

Designing a Low-Maintenance Chiltern Garden

Reduce the lawn

Lawns are the highest maintenance part of any garden. Reducing lawn area and replacing with beds of low-maintenance shrubs and perennials cuts maintenance dramatically. A bed of hardy geraniums and salvia takes 30 minutes a year to maintain. The same area of lawn takes 30 minutes a week in summer.

Use ground cover

Plant ground cover plants like hardy geraniums, bergenia, epimedium and ajuga between larger plants. These suppress weeds and reduce the need for mulching.

Plant in drifts

Planting 5 of the same variety together gives more impact than 5 different plants scattered. It also means when one plant finishes flowering, the whole drift has the same impact.

Choose long-flowering plants

Some plants flower for 2 weeks, others for 4 months. Choose plants that give long lasting colour: salvias, hardy geraniums, nepeta, verbena, agapanthus, japanese anemones. A border of long-flowering plants needs less deadheading and looks good for longer.

Mulch annually

A 5cm mulch of bark or compost in spring suppresses weeds for the whole growing season and reduces watering. It is the single most effective low-maintenance thing you can do.

Install irrigation for containers

If you have lots of pots, install a drip irrigation system on a timer. This takes 2 hours to set up and saves hours of watering each week through summer.

Common Mistakes

Planting too close

Every plant has a mature size. Read the label and plant accordingly. Planting too close means constant pruning and crowding.

Not improving the soil

Even drought-tolerant plants need good soil to establish. Add compost at planting time and mulch annually.

Buying on impulse

Garden centres are designed to make you buy what is in flower now. Plan your plant choices for year round interest, not just what looks good on the day you visit.

Ignoring mature size

A pittosporum in a 2 litre pot looks small. In 5 years it will be 2 metres tall and wide. Plan for the mature size.

Final Thoughts

A low-maintenance garden is not a no-maintenance garden. Even the easiest plants need watering through the first season, an annual mulch, and occasional pruning. But choosing the right plants for the Chiltern chalk makes a huge difference.

Plants that fight the conditions need constant attention. Plants that suit the conditions thrive with very little help. The ten plants in this list are the ones we plant most often for clients who want a beautiful garden without becoming a slave to it.
