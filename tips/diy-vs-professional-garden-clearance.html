Is It Cheaper to Clear a Garden Yourself or Hire Someone?

It is tempting to think that doing garden clearance yourself will save money. Sometimes it does. Often it does not. This guide compares the real costs, both financial and in time, of DIY clearance versus hiring a professional. The answer may surprise you.

The short answer

Quick Answer

For small, light clearances (under 50 square metres of moderate overgrowth), DIY is usually cheaper if you have the time and tools. For larger or heavily overgrown gardens, hiring a professional is often cheaper in real terms when you factor in tool hire, waste disposal, and the value of your own time.

The Hidden Costs of DIY

Tool hire

To clear a moderately overgrown garden you need:

  • Brush cutter or strimmer: 50 to 80 per day from a hire shop
  • Hedge trimmer: 30 to 50 per day
  • Wheelbarrow: 20 to 40 per day
  • Mattock and loppers: 30 to 60 to buy if you do not have them
  • Protective gear: gloves, goggles, ear defenders: 30 to 60 to buy
  • Waste bags or hippobag: 10 to 150

For a 2-day clearance, tool hire alone can come to 200 to 350.

Waste disposal

This is the big hidden cost. Green waste disposal is regulated. You cannot just put it in your black bin. Options:

  • Council tip: free for household waste but most tips limit you to 6 bags per visit. A typical clearance produces 20 to 40 bags. That means 4 to 7 trips.
  • Council green waste bin: 60 to 100 per year subscription. Takes months to clear a clearance worth of waste.
  • Hippobag: 150 to 250 per megabag including collection. Each bag holds about 1 tonne.
  • Skip hire: 200 to 400 for a 6 yard skip. Need a permit if on the road, 30 to 80.

For a typical clearance, waste disposal alone costs 150 to 400 if you do it yourself.

Your time

This is the cost most people ignore. A professional 2-person crew can clear a 100 square metre moderately overgrown garden in 1.5 days. That is 24 person-hours of work. If you do it alone, it will take you 4 to 6 days of hard physical work.

What is your time worth? If you are paid 20 pounds an hour, 6 days of work is nearly 1,000 pounds of your time. If you would rather spend that 6 days with family, on hobbies, or just relaxing, the cost is even higher.

Physical cost

Garden clearance is hard physical work. Brambles cut you. Ivy is heavy. Soil and rubble break your back. Most people who do a full clearance themselves end up with cuts, blisters, aching muscles, and sometimes worse. Back injuries from heavy lifting are common.

If you are not used to physical work, budget 2 to 3 times longer than you think. And accept that you will be useless for anything else for a few days after.

Pest and disease risks

Overgrown gardens harbour rats, ticks, and wasps. Weil's disease from rat urine in stagnant water can be fatal. Tick bites can cause Lyme disease. Wasp stings can be dangerous if you are allergic. Professionals know how to spot and avoid these risks.

When DIY Makes Sense

Small gardens

If your garden is small and the overgrowth is light, DIY is genuinely cheaper. A few hours with a pair of secateurs and a trip to the tip is all you need.

You have the tools already

If you already own a strimmer, hedge trimmer, wheelbarrow and the basic tools, the cost of DIY drops significantly. Most of the cost of a one-off clearance is tool hire.

You have the time and fitness

If you are retired, between jobs, or just enjoy hard physical work, the time cost is less of an issue. DIY becomes a reasonable choice.

You have a council green waste bin

If you have a council green waste bin and can clear the garden over a few months, the waste disposal cost drops to nearly nothing. You trade time for money.

You want the exercise

Garden clearance is genuinely good exercise if you are fit. Some people enjoy the satisfaction of seeing a transformation they did themselves.

When Hiring a Professional Makes Sense

Large or heavily overgrown gardens

The bigger the job, the more sense it makes to hire someone. Professionals have the equipment, the experience, and the crew to do in 2 days what would take you 2 weeks.

You need waste removal

If the garden has lots of green waste, hiring a professional with a tipper truck is much easier than multiple trips to the tip. They will also take the waste away for you.

Time pressure

If you are selling the house, moving in, or just want the garden usable for a specific date, hiring someone is the only realistic option. Most people cannot take 2 weeks off work to clear a garden.

Physical limitations

If you have any physical limitation, bad back, or are simply not used to physical work, do not attempt a full garden clearance. The risk of injury is high.

Large shrubs and small trees

Anything with a trunk over 10cm diameter needs proper equipment to remove. Hand saws work but take forever. Chain saws are dangerous in untrained hands.

Asbestos or hazardous waste

If the garden has an old shed with an asbestos roof, or chemicals, or anything you are not sure about, hire a professional. Asbestos removal is a specialist job.

The Halfway Option

There is a middle ground. You can do the easy bits yourself and hire a professional for the hard bits. For example:

  • You cut back the light overgrowth and the professional handles the brambles and trees
  • You do the clearance but hire a waste removal company to take the waste away
  • You clear the garden but hire someone to do the pressure washing and patio work

This can save money while still getting the hard work done professionally. We are happy to quote for part of a job.

Common DIY Mistakes

Underestimating the waste

Most DIY clearances produce 3 to 5 times more waste than people expect. A "small pile of brambles" turns into 15 bin bags. Always have more waste bags than you think you need.

Digging out weed roots too shallow

Perennial weeds like bindweed and ground elder regrow from any root fragment left in the soil. Digging out the top growth is pointless. You need to dig out every piece of root, often 30cm deep.

Cutting into old wood on leylandii

Leylandii hedges do not regrow from old brown wood. If you cut too hard, the hedge is permanently bare on that side. See our hedge cutting guide.

Pulling ivy off walls

Ivy holds on with tiny roots that damage mortar. Pulling live ivy off a wall can pull off the render or damage the mortar. Cut the stems at the base and let the top growth die before removing.

Ignoring nesting birds

If you cut hedges or shrubs during nesting season (March to August) you can be prosecuted. Always check first.

Final Thoughts

DIY garden clearance is not always cheaper than hiring a professional. For small light clearances, yes. For anything bigger, the cost of tool hire, waste disposal, and your own time often exceeds what a professional would charge.

The question is rarely "can I afford to hire someone?" It is more often "can I afford to do it myself?" Factor in your time, your fitness, the equipment you would need, and the cost of waste disposal.
