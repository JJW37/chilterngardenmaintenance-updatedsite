The short answer

Quick Answer

To build better borders on chalky ground: dig in plenty of organic matter, add a 5cm layer of compost annually, choose chalk-tolerant plants (lavender, rosemary, salvia, alliums, beech, yew), mulch thickly to retain moisture, and avoid acid-loving plants (rhododendrons, camellias). Chalky Chiltern soils can produce stunning borders if you work with the soil rather than against it.

Why Chalky Soil Is Challenging

Chalky soil is the dominant soil type across the Chilterns, from High Wycombe to Princes Risborough, Wendover, Amersham, Marlow and Henley. It is alkaline (pH 7.5 to 8.5), free-draining, and often shallow, with the topsoil just 10 to 20cm deep over solid chalk. These conditions make gardening challenging but not impossible.

The main challenges of chalky soil are:

  • Shallow topsoil: less room for roots
  • Free-draining: dries out fast in summer
  • Alkaline pH: locks up iron and other nutrients
  • Low organic matter: poor soil structure
  • Stony: hard to dig

The good news is that chalky soil suits a wide range of plants, particularly Mediterranean plants, herbs, and drought-tolerant perennials. With the right approach, chalky ground can produce stunning borders.

Step 1: Improve the Soil

Before planting, improve the soil. This is the single most important step.

  • Add organic matter: dig in 5 to 10cm of compost or well-rotted manure. This improves soil structure, water retention, and nutrient content.
  • Add grit if needed: on heavy chalk, add horticultural grit to improve drainage further.
  • Test the pH: chalky soil is pH 7.5 to 8.5. Do not try to lower it. Work with the alkalinity.
  • Remove large stones: dig out chalk boulders that will block roots.
  • Build raised beds: if the topsoil is very thin, build raised beds and fill with imported topsoil.

Step 2: Choose the Right Plants

Chalky soil suits a wide range of plants. The key is to choose plants that thrive in alkaline, free-draining conditions.

Best herbs for chalky soil

  • Lavender, the classic chalk plant
  • Rosemary, evergreen, drought-tolerant
  • Salvia, long-flowering, aromatic
  • Thyme, low ground cover
  • Sage, silvery foliage, evergreen

Best perennials for chalky soil

  • Alliums, dramatic spherical flowers
  • Hardy geraniums, long-flowering ground cover
  • Verbena bonariensis, tall, airy
  • Nepeta, blue flowers, grey foliage
  • Echinacea, pink daisy flowers
  • Sedum, late flowers for bees

Best shrubs for chalky soil

  • Photinia, red new growth, evergreen
  • Choisya, glossy leaves, scented flowers
  • Pittosporum, evergreen structure
  • Roses, thrive on chalk if well fed
  • Ceanothus, blue flowers in spring

Best hedges for chalky soil

  • Beech, the classic Chiltern hedge
  • Yew, dense, formal, long-lived
  • Privet, fast-growing, semi-evergreen
  • Holly, evergreen, slow-growing

Plants to avoid on chalky soil

  • Rhododendrons (need acid soil)
  • Camellias (need acid soil)
  • Pieris (need acid soil)
  • Blueberries (need acid soil)
  • Heathers (most varieties need acid soil)
  • Magnolias (most varieties need acid soil)

Step 3: Plant Correctly

Planting on chalky soil needs a slightly different approach:

  1. Dig a generous hole: twice the width of the pot, and 1.5 times as deep.
  2. Add compost to the hole: mix compost with the soil you dig out.
  3. Plant slightly proud: on shallow chalk, plant the rootball 2 to 3cm above the surrounding soil to avoid crown rot.
  4. Water thoroughly: soak the plant in its pot, then water again after planting.
  5. Mulch thickly: 5 to 7cm of bark or compost around the plant.
  6. Water weekly for the first season: chalk dries out fast, so new plants need regular watering.

Step 4: Mulch Annually

Annual mulching is essential on chalky soil. It:

  • Retains moisture (chalk dries out fast)
  • Suppresses weeds
  • Adds organic matter to the soil
  • Improves soil structure
  • Protects roots from heat and cold

Apply a 5cm layer of bark or compost in spring, when the soil is moist. See our mulching guide.

Step 5: Water and Feed

Chalky soil needs more watering than clay, because it drains fast. Water new plants weekly for the first growing season. After that, drought-tolerant plants should be self-sufficient.

Feed chalky soil borders in spring with a balanced fertiliser. Avoid high-nitrogen feeds, which produce soft growth vulnerable to drought. Choose a feed with iron for chalky soils, as iron is often locked up by the high pH.

Common Mistakes

  • Planting acid-lovers on chalk. Rhododendrons, camellias and blueberries will die. Use pots of ericaceous compost instead.
  • Not watering new plants. Chalk drains fast. New plants need weekly watering for the first growing season.
  • Not mulching. Chalk soil bakes hard in summer. Mulch keeps moisture in and weeds down.
  • Planting too deep. On shallow chalk, plant slightly proud to avoid crown rot.
  • Not improving the soil. Chalky soil is low in organic matter. Add compost annually.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I grow vegetables on chalky soil?

Yes, with preparation. Add plenty of compost and use raised beds for shallow soils. Most vegetables do well except potatoes (which scab) and brassicas (which prefer slightly acid soil).

How do I know if my soil is chalky?

Dig a small hole 30cm deep. If the soil is pale, contains white fragments, and fizzes when you add vinegar, it is chalky. A pH test will confirm pH 7.5 or above.

Will lavender grow on chalky soil?

Yes, lavender loves chalk. It is one of the best plants for chalky Chiltern gardens.

Estate Note

In established gardens, soil structure takes decades to build. Avoid deep cultivation near mature trees and shrubs, as their fine feeder roots extend well beyond the canopy.
