How to Mulch Properly and What to Buy

Mulching is the single most beneficial thing you can do for your garden. It suppresses weeds, retains moisture, improves soil and makes everything look tidy. But there are many types of mulch, and using the wrong one wastes money. This guide explains how to mulch properly and what to buy.

The short answer

Quick Answer

To mulch properly: apply a 5cm layer of bark or compost in spring when the soil is moist, keep a 5cm gap around plant stems, and reapply annually. The best mulches for Chiltern gardens are bark (for ornamental borders), compost (for vegetables and roses), and gravel (for Mediterranean plants). Cost: 30 to 100 pounds for a typical 50 sqm border.

Why Mulching Matters

Mulch is the single most effective thing you can do for your garden. A 5cm layer of bark or compost reduces evaporation by 70 percent, suppresses weeds by 80 percent, and adds organic matter to the soil. It is cheap, easy to apply, and makes a huge difference to plant health.

In the Chilterns, where the chalky soils dry out fast and the clay soils become compacted, mulch is essential. On chalk, mulch retains moisture and builds soil depth. On clay, mulch improves structure and drainage. This guide explains how to mulch properly and what to buy.

The Benefits of Mulching

  • Retains moisture: reduces evaporation by 70 percent
  • Suppresses weeds: blocks light, prevents germination
  • Adds organic matter: as it breaks down, it feeds the soil
  • Improves soil structure: especially on clay soils
  • Protects roots: from heat in summer and frost in winter
  • Looks good: a fresh mulch makes the garden look cared for
  • Encourages worms: worms feed on the mulch and aerate the soil

Types of Mulch

Bark

The most popular mulch for ornamental borders. Long-lasting, looks good, and suppresses weeds well. Cost: 5 to 10 pounds per 50 litre bag (covers about 1 square metre at 5cm depth).

  • Ornamental bark: chunky, looks good, lasts 2 to 3 years
  • Composted bark: finer, breaks down faster, feeds the soil
  • Bark fines: very fine, good for paths and play areas

Compost

Best for vegetables, roses, and hungry plants. Feeds the soil as well as mulching. Cost: 4 to 8 pounds per 50 litre bag.

  • Garden compost: free if you make it
  • Well-rotted manure: rich in nutrients, good for roses and vegetables
  • Mushroom compost: alkaline, good for chalky soils (avoid on acid soils)
  • Bagged compost: from garden centres, consistent quality

Gravel

Best for Mediterranean plants and gravel gardens. Permanent, looks good, but does not feed the soil. Cost: 5 to 10 pounds per 25kg bag.

  • 10 to 20mm angular gravel: best for gravel gardens
  • Cotswold chippings: golden colour, looks good in Chiltern gardens
  • Slate chippings: dark, modern look

Leaf Mould

Free if you make it. Best for woodland plants. Takes 1 to 2 years to make.

Straw

Best for strawberries and vegetables. Cheap, but blows around in wind.

Grass Clippings

Free if you have a lawn. Good for vegetables, but can smell as they break down. Apply in thin layers.

How to Mulch Properly

  1. Weed first: remove all weeds before mulching. Mulch suppresses new weeds but does not kill existing ones.
  2. Water first: water the soil thoroughly before mulching. Mulch locks in moisture, so the soil should be moist first.
  3. Apply 5cm: a 5cm layer is enough. More than 7cm can smother plant roots.
  4. Keep a gap around stems: leave a 5cm gap around plant stems to prevent rot.
  5. Do not pile against tree trunks: keep a 10cm gap around tree trunks.
  6. Reapply annually: bark and compost break down and need reapplying each year.

When to Mulch

The best time to mulch is mid-spring (March to April), when the soil is moist and warming up. Avoid mulching in summer (the soil is too dry) or winter (the soil is too cold).

Autumn mulching is also effective, as it protects the soil from winter rain and frost. Apply after the autumn tidy-up.

Common Mistakes

  • Not weeding first. Mulch suppresses new weeds but does not kill existing ones. Weed first.
  • Not watering first. Mulch locks in moisture, so the soil should be moist first.
  • Applying too thickly. More than 7cm can smother plant roots. 5cm is enough.
  • Piling mulch against stems. This causes rot. Keep a 5cm gap around stems.
  • Using the wrong mulch. Use bark for ornamentals, compost for vegetables, gravel for Mediterranean plants.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best mulch for Chiltern gardens?

Bark for ornamental borders, compost for vegetables and roses, gravel for Mediterranean plants. On chalky soils, all three work well.

How often should I mulch?

Annually, in spring. Bark and compost break down and need reapplying each year. Gravel is permanent.

Can I use grass clippings as mulch?

Yes, but apply in thin layers (1 to 2cm) to prevent smell. Good for vegetables, not for ornamentals.
