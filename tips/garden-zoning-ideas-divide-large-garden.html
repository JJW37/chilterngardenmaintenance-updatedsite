The short answer

Quick Answer

Garden zoning divides a large garden into distinct areas, each with a different purpose. The best zones are: formal garden near the house, family garden (lawn and play), ornamental borders, vegetable garden, wildlife area, and woodland edge. Use hedges, paths, changes of level, and planting to define the zones. In the Chilterns, zoning is essential to make a large garden feel manageable.

Why Zoning Matters

A large garden without zones is just a field. The same garden, divided into zones, becomes a series of distinct spaces each with its own character and purpose. Zoning makes a large garden feel manageable, usable and interesting.

The human eye likes to be led. A garden that is all one thing (all lawn, all border, all meadow) is boring. A garden that is divided into distinct spaces, each revealed as you walk through, is engaging. Zoning also makes maintenance easier, because each zone can be managed differently.

In the Chilterns, where large gardens range from 0.5 to 5 acres, zoning is essential. This guide explains how to zone a large garden, with specific ideas for Chiltern conditions.

The Standard Zone Layout

A typical large garden has 4 to 6 zones, arranged from the house outwards:

Zone 1: Formal Garden (near the house)

The most visible and most maintained part of the garden. Includes:

  • Patio or terrace for sitting
  • Formal lawn with crisp edges
  • Neat borders with shrubs and perennials
  • Containers by the doors

Zone 2: Family Garden

Functional and durable, for everyday use:

  • Larger lawn for children to play
  • Vegetable garden or raised beds
  • Children's play area (swings, trampoline)
  • Shed and utility area

Zone 3: Ornamental Garden

The decorative heart of the garden:

  • Mixed borders with shrubs, perennials, bulbs
  • Specimen trees
  • Garden room or summerhouse
  • Water feature or pond

Zone 4: Wild Garden

Low maintenance, high wildlife value:

  • Wildflower meadow
  • Orchard
  • Woodland edge
  • Pond or bog garden

Zone 5: Boundary

Screening and shelter:

  • Hedges for privacy
  • Belt of trees for windbreak
  • Wildlife corridor

How to Define Zones

Hedges

Hedges are the best way to define zones. They provide a physical and visual barrier without being too solid. Use:

  • Beech for chalk soils
  • Hornbeam for clay soils
  • Yew for formal hedges
  • Hawthorn for informal and wildlife hedges

See our hedging guide.

Paths

Paths connect the zones and lead the eye. Use different materials for different zones:

  • Stone paving near the house (formal)
  • Gravel for informal paths
  • Grass for wild areas
  • Bark for woodland paths

Changes of Level

If your garden slopes, use changes of level to define zones. Terraces, retaining walls, and steps all create distinct spaces. A change of level of just 30cm is enough to make a zone feel separate.

Planting

Planting can define zones without hard landscaping. A swathe of ornamental grasses separates one zone from another. A row of specimen trees marks a boundary. A change from formal planting to naturalistic planting signals a change of zone.

Focal Points

Each zone needs a focal point, something that draws the eye and gives the space a purpose:

  • A statue in the formal garden
  • A specimen tree in the ornamental garden
  • A pond in the wild garden
  • A bench at the end of a path
  • A garden building

Zone Design Ideas for Chiltern Gardens

The Chilterns Hillside Garden

For gardens on the chalk hills around High Wycombe, Princes Risborough and Wendover:

  • Zone 1: Formal terrace near the house, with Mediterranean planting
  • Zone 2: Lawn and borders on the level ground
  • Zone 3: Gravel garden on the slope, with drought-tolerant plants
  • Zone 4: Wildflower meadow at the bottom, on the thinner soil
  • Zone 5: Native hedge at the boundary

The Vale Garden

For gardens on clay in the Vale of Aylesbury:

  • Zone 1: Formal lawn and borders
  • Zone 2: Vegetable garden with raised beds
  • Zone 3: Mixed borders with moisture-tolerant plants
  • Zone 4: Pond and bog garden
  • Zone 5: Native hedge and woodland edge

The Riverside Garden

For gardens near the Thames in Reading, Wallingford and Henley:

  • Zone 1: Raised terrace (in case of flooding)
  • Zone 2: Lawn on higher ground
  • Zone 3: Borders with moisture-tolerant plants
  • Zone 4: Bog garden and pond
  • Zone 5: Riverside planting

Common Mistakes

  • Not having a zoning plan. A large garden without zones is just a field. Get a design before you start.
  • Too many zones. 4 to 6 zones is enough. More than that feels fragmented.
  • Zones that are too small. Each zone needs to be big enough to feel like a distinct space.
  • No connection between zones. Zones need paths, views, and structure to connect them.
  • Not using the natural features. Use slopes, views, and existing trees to define zones.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many zones should a large garden have?

4 to 6 is typical. Fewer than 4 feels undefined; more than 6 feels fragmented.

How do I connect the zones?

Use paths, views, hedges, and planting. A path should lead from one zone to another, with a focal point at the end.

Should I hire a designer for zoning?

Yes, for a large garden. A designer saves money by avoiding mistakes and creates a cohesive plan. See our garden design service.
