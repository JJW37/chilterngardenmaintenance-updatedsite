Garden Drainage Problems: Signs You Need Professional Help

Poor drainage is one of the most common garden problems in the UK, especially on heavy clay soils. But how do you know when it is serious enough to need professional help? This guide lists the signs that your garden has a drainage problem that needs a professional.

The short answer

Quick Answer

Signs you need professional help with garden drainage: water sits on the surface for more than 24 hours after rain, the lawn is mossy and waterlogged, paths and patios flood, plants are dying from root rot, and the soil smells sour. Solutions include land drains, soakaways, and improving soil structure. In the Chilterns, drainage problems are most common on clay soils in the Vale of Aylesbury and around Didcot.

When to Call a Professional

Some garden problems are DIY jobs. Others need a professional. Garden drainage is in the second category. Installing land drains, soakaways and drainage systems is hard work, requires specific knowledge, and is often regulated. If you have persistent drainage problems, call a professional.

In the Chilterns, drainage problems are most common on the heavy clay soils of the Vale of Aylesbury, around Didcot and Bicester. The clay holds water for days after rain, and the soil can become waterlogged for weeks in winter. Properties in low-lying areas near the Thames (Reading, Wallingford, Henley) are also vulnerable when the river is high.

The 7 Signs You Need Professional Drainage Help

1. Water Sits on the Surface for More Than 24 Hours

Normal soil drains within a few hours of rain. If water is still sitting on the surface 24 hours after rain, you have a drainage problem. This is usually caused by compaction or a high water table.

2. The Lawn Is Mossy and Waterlogged

Moss thrives in wet, compacted conditions. A mossy lawn is a sign of poor drainage. You can treat the moss (see our moss guide), but if you do not fix the drainage, the moss will return.

3. Paths and Patios Flood

If your paths and patios flood after rain, you have a drainage problem. This may be caused by runoff from the garden, poor fall on the paving, or blocked drains.

4. Plants Are Dying from Root Rot

If plants are dying despite adequate watering, the cause may be root rot from waterlogged soil. Symptoms include yellowing leaves, wilting, and a sour smell from the soil. Check the soil moisture by digging a small hole.

5. The Soil Smells Sour

Waterlogged soil becomes anaerobic (lacking oxygen), which produces a sour, rotten smell. If your soil smells sour, it is waterlogged and the plants are suffocating.

6. Standing Water in Winter

Some standing water in winter is normal. But if it persists for more than a week, or if it returns every winter, you have a drainage problem.

7. Neighbours Have Drainage Problems Too

If your neighbours have drainage problems, you probably will too. Drainage problems are often caused by the local soil and topography, affecting multiple properties.

What a Professional Drainage Contractor Does

1. Survey the Garden

A drainage contractor will survey the garden to identify the cause of the problem. This may include:

  • Digging trial holes to check soil type and water table
  • Checking existing drains
  • Assessing the slope and fall of the garden
  • Identifying sources of runoff

2. Recommend a Solution

Based on the survey, the contractor will recommend a solution. Options include:

  • Land drains: perforated pipes in gravel trenches to collect and carry water away
  • Soakaway: a hole filled with rubble or crates to absorb water
  • Surface drains: channels and gullies to collect surface water
  • Rain garden: a planted depression to absorb runoff
  • Soil improvement: adding organic matter to improve drainage
  • Aeration: relieving compaction in lawns

3. Install the Solution

The contractor will install the recommended solution. This usually involves:

  • Digging trenches for land drains
  • Digging a hole for a soakaway
  • Installing pipes and connections
  • Backfilling and reinstating the surface
  • Testing the system

How to Choose a Drainage Contractor

  • Get at least 3 quotes. Prices and recommendations vary widely.
  • Check insurance. Public liability insurance is essential.
  • Ask for references. A good contractor has references from previous clients.
  • Get a written quote. Including exactly what is included and what is not.
  • Avoid contractors who want to dig before surveying. A proper survey is essential.
  • Check waste disposal. Soil from drainage work must be disposed of legally.

Common Mistakes

  • Not identifying the cause. Installing a soakaway without fixing compaction is a waste of money. Diagnose first.
  • Connecting land drains to the sewer. This is illegal in most areas. Land drains must go to a soakaway or surface water drain.
  • Building a soakaway too close to the house. At least 5 metres from the house, or you risk foundation damage.
  • Not maintaining drains. Land drains silt up over time. Lift inspection chambers annually and check flow.
  • Paving over the garden. This makes flooding worse, not better. Use permeable surfaces instead.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a land drain cost?

30 to 60 pounds per metre installed. A typical garden needs 10 to 30 metres, so 300 to 1,800 pounds.

Will aeration fix my flooded lawn?

If the flooding is caused by compaction, yes. If it is caused by a high water table or heavy clay, no. Aeration helps but does not solve underlying drainage problems.

Can I install a soakaway myself?

Yes, if you are fit and have a strong back. Dig a hole 1 to 2 metres deep, fill with rubble or plastic crates, and connect a pipe from your drain. Cost: 200 to 300 pounds in materials.
