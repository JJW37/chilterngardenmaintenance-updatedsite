Autumn Lawn Care Guide for UK Gardens

Autumn is when you do the serious work on a UK lawn. The grass is still growing, the soil is warm, and there is time for the lawn to recover before winter sets in. What you do in September and October will largely determine how the lawn looks the following spring and how well it survives the cold months. This guide covers scarifying, aerating, top dressing, overseeding, autumn feeding and leaf management, with a step by step process for a typical family lawn in southern England.

The short answer

Quick Answer

Autumn lawn care is about repairing the damage of summer and preparing the grass for winter. The four jobs that matter most are scarifying to remove thatch, aerating to relieve compaction, top dressing to improve the surface, and overseeding to thicken the sward. Apply an autumn feed that is low in nitrogen and high in potash. Keep the lawn clear of leaves through to the first frost.

If you only have time for one job, scarify. Removing the dead material that has built up over summer does more for the long term health of the lawn than anything else.

Why Autumn Is the Best Time

Most UK garden owners think of spring as the main lawn season, but autumn is when the real work happens. In autumn the soil is still warm from summer, but the air is cooler and there is more rain. Grass seed germinates reliably, the new roots have months to develop before the stress of summer, and there is less weed competition than in spring.

The other reason autumn matters is that the lawn has just come through a summer of mowing, foot traffic, and possibly drought. The thatch layer has built up, the soil is compacted, and there are bare patches. If you leave all this until spring, the lawn goes into winter weak and emerges in March thin and patchy.

The Autumn Window

The ideal window for autumn lawn work is mid September to mid October, while the soil is still warm enough for grass seed to germinate. Leave it until November and the seed will sit dormant until spring, by which time birds and heavy rain will have removed half of it.

The Full Autumn Renovation Process

Step 1: Mow the lawn short

A few days before you start, mow the lawn slightly shorter than usual, around 30mm. This makes scarifying easier and lets you see what you are doing. Do not scalp the lawn, just take it down a notch from the summer height.

Step 2: Scarify to remove thatch

Thatch is the layer of dead grass, moss and debris that sits at the base of the lawn, just above the soil. A thin layer of thatch, up to 1cm, is fine and helps retain moisture. More than that and it blocks water, air and fertiliser from reaching the soil.

For small lawns, use a spring tine rake. For lawns over 50 square metres, hire a powered scarifier for around 60 to 80 pounds per day. Set the scarifier to a moderate depth to start. You can always go over again. The aim is to pull out dead material, not to tear up the living grass.

A properly scarified lawn looks terrible for a few days. Do not panic. A 50 square metre lawn can produce 4 to 6 bin bags of thatch and dead moss. The grass will recover within a fortnight if the weather is mild.

Step 3: Aerate to relieve compaction

Compaction is the silent killer of lawns. The soil gets compressed by foot traffic, mowing, and summer use, squeezing out the air pockets that roots need. Compacted lawns drain badly, root shallowly, and are prone to moss.

For small lawns, push a garden fork into the soil 10cm deep every 10cm and rock it back and forth. For larger lawns, hire a hollow tine aerator. Hollow tining removes plugs of soil, which is more effective than solid tining. Brush a 70/30 sand and soil mix into the holes to keep them open.

Step 4: Top dress to level and improve the surface

Top dressing is a mix of sandy loam that you spread thinly over the lawn after aerating. It fills the aeration holes, levels minor dips, and improves the soil structure over time. The standard mix is 70 percent sharp sand and 30 percent sandy loam, or 60/30/10 with compost added.

Apply around 3 to 4kg per square metre, no more than 1cm thick. Work it in with the back of a rake or a stiff brush. The grass should still be visible through the dressing. If you bury the grass, it will not recover.

Step 5: Overseed bare and thin patches

After scarifying and top dressing, the lawn will look thin. Overseed at 20 to 25g per square metre across the whole lawn, or double that rate on bare patches. Use a seed mix suited to your conditions. A hard wearing ryegrass mix is good for family lawns, a fescue and bent mix is better for ornamental lawns, and a shade tolerant mix for shaded areas.

Lightly rake the seed into the top dressing, firm with a roller or by walking on it, and water if no rain is forecast. Keep the surface moist until the seed germinates, usually 10 to 14 days in September.

Step 6: Apply an autumn feed

Autumn feeds are different from spring feeds. They are low in nitrogen, which would produce soft growth that winter frost would damage, and high in potash, which strengthens cell walls and helps the grass resist cold and disease. Apply at the rate on the packet when the soil is moist and rain is forecast.

Do not use a spring and summer feed in autumn. The high nitrogen content will push the grass into soft growth that is exactly what you do not want going into winter.

Light Renovation Versus Full Renovation

Not every lawn needs the full process above. For a lawn that is basically healthy but a bit tired, a light renovation is enough. This involves a light scarify with a rake, a fork aeration of the worst areas, and an autumn feed. Skip the top dressing and overseed only the obviously bare patches.

A full renovation is needed if the lawn has more than 20 percent bare patches, more than 1cm of thatch, visible compaction, or heavy moss. For lawns that are more than half moss or have failed over several seasons, see our moss in lawns guide and consider a full returf or reseed.

Leaf Management

Falling leaves are the single biggest threat to an autumn lawn. A layer of leaves left on the grass for more than a week blocks light, encourages worm activity, and creates the damp, dark conditions that moss loves. In a bad year, a layer of leaves left for a month can kill the grass underneath.

Clear leaves weekly from late September through to early December. Use a leaf blower or a lawn mower with the collector on. The mower chops the leaves and collects them in one pass, which is much faster than raking. Add the chopped leaves to the compost heap or use them as leaf mould.

Beech and oak leaves take longer to break down than most. If you have a lot of these, make a separate leaf mould pile in a corner of the garden. Put the leaves in a simple wire mesh cage and leave them for two years. The resulting leaf mould is an excellent soil improver.

Autumn Mowing

Keep mowing through autumn as long as the grass is growing, but raise the mower gradually. Mow at 40mm in September, 45mm in October, and give a final cut at 50mm in November if the weather is mild. The last cut should be when growth has slowed to the point where you are only topping the grass.

Do not mow when the grass is wet or frosty. Wet mowing tears the grass and compacts the soil. Frost mowing can break the frozen grass blades and damage the crown of the plant. If the lawn needs tidying in winter, use a brush or leaf blower rather than the mower.

Last Cut of the Year

The last cut of the year is usually in late October or early November depending on where you are in the UK. In the Chilterns, which tend to be a bit colder than the Thames Valley, the last cut is typically early November. After this, keep off the lawn as much as possible through winter.

Wet Soil Warning

If your soil is waterlogged after autumn rain, stay off the lawn entirely. Walking on a saturated lawn compacts the soil and damages the grass crowns. Wait until the surface has drained, even if it means falling behind on leaf clearance.

Dealing With Worm Casts

Worm casts are the small piles of soil that worms leave on the surface of the lawn in autumn and winter. They are a sign of healthy soil, but they are unsightly and can smear when mowed. The simplest solution is to wait for the casts to dry and then sweep them with a stiff brush. Do this on a dry morning before they have time to harden.

There are no worm killers legally available to home gardeners in the UK, and rightly so. Worms are good for your soil. If the casts are a serious problem, reduce the pH slightly by avoiding lime and using acidifying fertilisers such as ammonium sulphate in autumn. This will not eliminate worms but may reduce activity on the surface.

Autumn Lawn Problems to Watch For

Leatherjackets

Leatherjackets are the larvae of crane flies, or daddy longlegs. They feed on grass roots in autumn and can leave the lawn yellow and loose. If you can peel back the turf easily, you have leatherjackets. The biological control with nematodes is effective if applied in autumn while the soil is still warm. See the RHS guidance on leatherjackets.

Chafer grubs

Chafer grubs are the larvae of chafer beetles. They are larger than leatherjackets and feed on grass roots in late summer and autumn. Birds, particularly crows and magpies, will tear up the lawn looking for them. The damage can be extensive. Nematode treatment is effective but timing is critical.

Fusarium patch

Fusarium patch, also known as snow mould, appears as small circular patches of pinkish or white dead grass in late autumn and winter. It is encouraged by poor drainage, thatch, and excessive autumn nitrogen. Scarletify, aerate, and avoid high nitrogen feeds in autumn. If fusarium appears, lightly brush the dew off the lawn in the morning.

Tools and Materials You Will Need

For a full autumn renovation of a 50 square metre lawn you will need: a spring tine rake or powered scarifier, a garden fork or hollow tine aerator, 4 to 5 bags of top dressing (around 150 to 200kg), 1 to 1.5kg of grass seed, a 2kg box of autumn fertiliser, and a stiff brush for working in the top dressing.

Total material cost for a 50 square metre lawn is around 80 to 120 pounds if you do it yourself. Hiring a powered scarifier adds another 60 to 80 pounds per day. Professional autumn renovation typically costs 200 to 400 pounds for a similar sized lawn, depending on the work involved.

When to Call a Professional

If your lawn has been failing for several years, has heavy moss, deep compaction, or extensive bare patches, a professional renovation is worth it. Autumn renovation is also worth booking if you are time poor. The work needs to be done in a tight window in September and October, and it is physical work. A professional team can do in a morning what would take a homeowner a full weekend.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I scarify in spring instead of autumn?

You can, but it is not ideal. Spring scarifying weakens the grass just as it is trying to grow, and the lawn takes longer to recover. Light raking in spring is fine, but heavy scarifying is best done in autumn when the grass has the whole winter to recover.

Do I need to top dress every year?

No. Top dressing once every two or three years is enough for most lawns. If your lawn is uneven or has heavy compaction, you may want to top dress annually for the first couple of years, then drop to every other year.

Will grass seed germinate in October?

In a mild autumn, yes. Grass seed germinates reliably at soil temperatures above 7 Celsius. By mid October the soil is usually still warm enough in southern England, but seed sown in November will probably sit dormant until spring. If in doubt, sow by mid October.

What happens if I skip autumn lawn care?

The thatch builds up, the soil stays compacted, and the moss and weeds move in. The lawn goes into winter weak and emerges in spring thin and patchy. You will spend the following spring trying to fix problems that would have been easier to fix in autumn.

Final Thoughts

Autumn is the season when UK lawns are made or broken. The grass is still growing, the soil is warm, and the conditions are right for repair work. A weekend of scarifying, aerating, top dressing and overseeding in September will give you a far better lawn the following spring than any amount of spring work.
