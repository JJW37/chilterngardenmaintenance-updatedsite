Should You Leave Part of Your Garden Wild?

Leaving part of your garden wild is one of the best things you can do for wildlife. But it needs to be done deliberately, not just by neglect. This guide explains the pros and cons of leaving part of your garden wild, and how to do it well.

The short answer

Quick Answer

Yes, you should leave part of your garden wild. A wild area supports 10 times more wildlife than a manicured lawn, requires minimal maintenance, and adds visual interest. Aim for 10 to 20 percent of the garden as a wild area, in the least visible corner. In the Chilterns, where gardens often back onto countryside, a wild area can be a vital wildlife corridor.

Why Leave Part of the Garden Wild

The UK has lost 97 percent of its wildflower meadows since 1930. Half of our native pollinators are in decline. Gardens cover 1 million hectares of the UK, more than all the nature reserves combined. What gardeners do matters. Leaving part of the garden wild is one of the most effective things you can do for wildlife.

The good news is that a wild area is not just good for wildlife. It is also low-maintenance, looks beautiful, and adds interest to the garden. A wild area at the bottom of the garden, with long grass and wildflowers, can be the most beautiful part of the garden in June and July.

In the Chilterns, where gardens often back onto countryside, a wild area can act as a wildlife corridor, connecting isolated patches of habitat. The more gardeners who leave wild areas, the more connected the landscape becomes.

The Benefits of a Wild Area

  • Supports 10 times more wildlife than a manicured lawn
  • Low maintenance: cut once a year, that is it
  • Beautiful: wildflowers in summer are stunning
  • Good for soil: long grass builds organic matter
  • Carbon storage: long grass stores more carbon than short grass
  • Pest control: a wild area supports predators that eat garden pests
  • Education: children love exploring wild areas
  • Privacy: a wild area screens the bottom of the garden

How Much to Leave Wild

Aim for 10 to 20 percent of the garden as a wild area. In a typical 300 sqm family garden, that is 30 to 60 sqm, or a 5 by 6 to 8 metre patch at the bottom of the garden.

You can leave more if you have a large garden. Estates of an acre or more can have 30 to 50 percent wild, often as a meadow or woodland edge.

Where to Put the Wild Area

Put the wild area in the least visible part of the garden, usually the bottom corner. This keeps the visible parts of the garden tidy while still providing wildlife habitat. Avoid putting the wild area in the front garden, where it may look untidy to passers-by and cause neighbour disputes.

How to Create a Wild Area

Option 1: Just Stop Mowing

The simplest option. Stop mowing a section of lawn and let the grass grow. Within a year, you will have a patch of long grass. Within 2 to 3 years, wildflowers will appear (from seeds in the soil). Within 5 years, you will have a mini meadow.

This is the lowest-effort option, but the results are unpredictable. You may get a beautiful wildflower meadow, or you may get a patch of coarse grass and nettles.

Option 2: Plant a Wildflower Meadow

For more reliable results, plant a wildflower meadow. See our wildflower meadow guide for detail. The basic process:

  1. Reduce soil fertility (wildflowers prefer poor soil). Stop fertilising for a year before planting.
  2. Sow wildflower seed in autumn or spring.
  3. Cut once a year in late summer (August to September).
  4. Remove the cuttings to keep the soil poor.

Cost: 50 to 150 pounds for seed for a 50 sqm meadow.

Option 3: Plant a Wildlife Corner

If you do not want long grass, plant a wildlife corner with native shrubs and perennials:

  • Hawthorn, flowers, berries, nesting sites
  • Blackthorn, sloes, nesting sites
  • Hazel, catkins, nuts
  • Foxglove, nectar for bees
  • Nettles, food for butterfly caterpillars

Add a log pile and a leaf pile for habitat. See our balance guide.

Option 4: Let Nature Take Over

If you have a large garden, you can simply fence off a section and let nature take over. Within 5 years, it will be a thicket of brambles, self-seeded trees, and nettles. Within 10 years, it will be a young woodland. This is the lowest-effort option but the most visually wild.

How to Manage a Wild Area

A wild area is not a no-maintenance area. It needs annual management to prevent it becoming a bramble thicket:

  • Meadow: cut once a year in late summer (August to September). Remove the cuttings.
  • Long grass: cut once a year in late summer. Remove the cuttings.
  • Wildlife corner: prune shrubs every 2 to 3 years. Remove invasive species.
  • Nature area: cut paths through it annually to allow access.

How to Make a Wild Area Look Intentional

A common complaint about wild areas is that they look messy. The trick is to make them look intentional:

  • Mow paths through the wild area. This shows it is deliberate.
  • Define the edges with a mown strip or edging.
  • Add a feature like a bench, sculpture, or bird bath.
  • Frame it with a hedge or fence.
  • Add a sign like "Wildflower Meadow" or "Wildlife Area".

Common Mistakes

  • Letting the whole garden go wild. This looks abandoned, not wildlife-friendly. Keep 10 to 20 percent wild, the rest maintained.
  • Not cutting the wild area annually. Without an annual cut, the wild area becomes a bramble thicket.
  • Not removing cuttings. Cuttings rot down and fertilise the soil, which favours grass over wildflowers.
  • Putting the wild area in the front garden. This can cause neighbour disputes. Keep wild areas in the back garden.
  • Not making it look intentional. A wild area without mown paths looks abandoned. Mow paths to show it is deliberate.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will a wild area annoy my neighbours?

Not if it is in the back garden and looks intentional. Mow paths through it and frame it with a hedge. The three-zone approach (formal near the house, wildlife-friendly in the middle, wild at the bottom) prevents neighbour disputes.

Will a wild area attract rats?

Not usually. Rats are attracted by food waste, not long grass. A wild area without food waste will not attract rats.

How long does it take for a wild area to establish?

1 to 3 years. The first year looks untidy, the second year looks promising, and by the third year it should look intentional and support a wide range of wildlife.

Estate Note

Mature gardens are often the richest wildlife habitats in an area. Before making changes, observe which species use the garden and at what times of year. Small adjustments can have outsized benefits.
