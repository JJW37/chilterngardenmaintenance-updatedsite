How to Reclaim an Overgrown Lawn and Border Area

When both the lawn and the borders have been left too long, the job is bigger than either one alone. But the process is the same: clear, improve, renovate, replant. This guide explains how to reclaim an overgrown lawn and border area in stages.

The short answer

Quick Answer

To reclaim an overgrown lawn and border: cut the lawn back gradually (never more than one third of the height in one cut), scarify to remove thatch and moss, aerate to relieve compaction, overseed bare patches, and clear the borders of weeds before mulching and replanting. In the Chilterns, the chalky soils dry out fast after clearing, so watering is essential for the first 6 weeks. A typical 50 sqm lawn and border rescue costs 300 to 700 pounds if we do it.

Why Lawns and Borders Get Overgrown

Lawns and borders get overgrown for predictable reasons: a year or two of missed maintenance, a tenant who did not look after the garden, an illness, or simply a busy period in life. The lawn gets long and mossy, the borders get weedy and overgrown, and the whole garden looks neglected. The good news is that almost any lawn and border can be rescued in a single season.

In the Chilterns, the chalky soils around High Wycombe and Amersham dry out fast, which stresses the lawn in summer. The clay soils in Aylesbury and Bicester hold water, which encourages moss. Both soil types need different rescue approaches. This guide explains how to reclaim an overgrown lawn and border, with specific notes for Chiltern conditions.

Step 1: Assess the Damage

Walk the lawn and borders and note:

  • Lawn height (how long is it?)
  • Moss coverage (percentage of the lawn)
  • Bare patches (size and location)
  • Weeds in the lawn (dandelions, daisies, plantain)
  • Border weeds (bindweed, couch grass, ground elder)
  • Overgrown shrubs and perennials
  • Soil type (chalk or clay)

Step 2: Reclaim the Lawn

1. Cut back gradually

If the lawn is very long (over 10cm), cut it back gradually over 2 to 3 cuts, a week apart. Never remove more than one third of the height in one cut, or you will shock the grass. Set the mower high for the first cut, then lower it gradually.

2. Treat moss

Apply iron sulphate at 5g per square metre to kill the moss. The moss will turn black within 2 weeks, ready for scarification. On chalky Chiltern soils, this mild acidification is beneficial as it helps unlock nutrients.

3. Scarify

Use a powered scarifier to remove dead moss and thatch. A 50 sqm lawn will produce 4 to 6 bin bags of material. The lawn will look bare and thin afterwards, but this is normal. See our scarification guide.

4. Aerate

Aerate to relieve compaction. Use a hollow-tine or solid-tine aerator, 10cm deep every 10cm. On clay soils in Aylesbury and Bicester, hollow-tine aeration is particularly important.

5. Overseed

Overseed bare and thin patches at 25g per square metre. Choose a seed mix suited to your conditions: shade-tolerant for shaded lawns, drought-tolerant for chalky soils. See our overseeding guide.

6. Feed

Apply a balanced fertiliser at 30g per square metre. Water in if no rain within 2 days.

Step 3: Reclaim the Borders

1. Cut back dead growth

Cut back all dead stems and seed heads from perennials and grasses. This makes it easier to see what you are doing.

2. Dig out perennial weeds

Dig out perennial weeds (bindweed, couch grass, ground elder) with a fork. Get every bit of root. See our bindweed guide.

3. Lift and divide perennials

Lift and divide overcrowded perennials. Replant the vigorous outer sections, discard the tired centre.

4. Prune overgrown shrubs

Prune overgrown shrubs to a manageable size. Most deciduous shrubs can be cut back hard in late winter. See our pruning guide.

5. Improve the soil

Add 5cm of compost and dig it in. On chalky soils, this builds depth. On clay soils, add grit as well to improve drainage.

6. Mulch

Apply a 5cm layer of bark or compost mulch to suppress weeds and retain moisture.

Common Mistakes

  • Cutting the lawn too short in one go. This shocks the grass and can kill it. Cut back gradually.
  • Not scarifying. Thatch and moss build up without annual scarification. The lawn declines.
  • Not digging out perennial weed roots. Pulling the tops leaves the roots, which regrow.
  • Not watering new seed. New grass seed needs daily watering for 3 weeks to germinate.
  • Not mulching after clearing. Bare soil is an invitation for weeds. Always mulch.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take to reclaim an overgrown lawn?

4 to 8 weeks for the lawn to recover. Full recovery takes 3 to 6 months, with the best results seen the following spring.

Should I returf or reseed?

Reseed if more than 50 percent of the lawn is salvageable. Returf if the lawn is beyond repair. See our turf vs seed guide.

When is the best time to reclaim a lawn?

Spring (March to May) or autumn (September to October), when the soil is warm and there is regular rain.

Estate Note

On mature lawns, check for leatherjackets and chafer grubs before scarifying. Established turf recovers more slowly than newly seeded areas, so adjust the renovation intensity accordingly.
