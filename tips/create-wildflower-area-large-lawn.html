How to Create a Wildflower Area in a Large Lawn

Converting part of a large lawn to a wildflower area is one of the best things you can do for wildlife and one of the most rewarding garden projects. But it needs to be done properly. This guide explains how to create a wildflower area in a large lawn.

Why Create a Wildflower Area

A wildflower meadow is one of the most beautiful and wildlife-friendly features you can create in a garden. It supports 50 times more pollinators than a manicured lawn, looks stunning in summer, and needs just one cut per year. For a large lawn, converting part of it to a wildflower meadow saves mowing time and adds huge wildlife value.

In the Chilterns, the chalky soils are perfect for native wildflowers. Cowslips, orchids, scabious, knapweed and bird's-foot trefoil all thrive on chalk. A wildflower meadow on chalk can be as beautiful as any traditional garden border, and far more valuable for wildlife.

The Benefits of a Wildflower Meadow

  • Supports 50 times more pollinators than a manicured lawn
  • Low maintenance: cut once a year, that is it
  • Beautiful: wildflowers in summer are stunning
  • Climate resilient: drought-tolerant once established
  • Good for soil: deep roots build soil structure
  • Carbon storage: meadows store more carbon than lawns
  • Saves mowing time: cut once a year vs weekly for a lawn

How to Create a Wildflower Meadow

Step 1: Choose the Right Spot

Wildflowers need sun. Choose a sunny spot, at least 6 hours of sun a day. Avoid shaded areas under trees. The spot should be in the least visible part of the garden, as the meadow will look untidy in winter and early spring.

Step 2: Reduce Soil Fertility

Wildflowers prefer poor soil. On rich soil, grass outcompetes the wildflowers. To reduce fertility:

  • Stop fertilising for 1 to 2 years before sowing
  • Remove the top 5 to 10cm of topsoil (the most fertile layer)
  • Or sow a crop of mustard (which takes nutrients from the soil) and dig it in

On chalky Chiltern soils, fertility is usually low enough already. On clay or rich garden soil, you will need to reduce fertility.

Step 3: Prepare the Seed Bed

Prepare a fine, weed-free seed bed:

  1. Kill existing vegetation (with glyphosate or by covering with black plastic for 3 months)
  2. Cultivate the soil to a fine tilth
  3. Remove weed roots and stones
  4. Firm the soil gently
  5. Rake to a fine surface

Step 4: Sow the Seed

Sow in autumn (September to November) or spring (March to April). Autumn sowing is best, as some wildflower seeds need cold to germinate.

  • Mix seed with sand (1 part seed to 4 parts sand) for even sowing
  • Sow at 4 to 5 grams per square metre
  • Rake lightly to cover the seed
  • Firm the soil with a roller or by walking on it
  • Water if no rain within a week

Choose a native wildflower mix suited to your soil. For chalk, choose a chalk grassland mix. For clay, choose a clay meadow mix. See our Chiltern Plant Library for suggestions.

Step 5: Manage the Meadow

Annual management is essential:

  • First year: cut the meadow every 6 to 8 weeks to control annual weeds. Remove the cuttings.
  • Subsequent years: cut once a year in late summer (August to September). Remove the cuttings.
  • Spot-treat weeds: dig out any invasive weeds (thistle, dock, nettle) as they appear.
  • Monitor for success: count the wildflower species each year. A good meadow has 20 to 30 species.

What Wildflowers to Sow

For chalky Chiltern soils:

  • Cowslip
  • Wild orchid (bee orchid, pyramidal orchid)
  • Scabious
  • Knapweed
  • Bird's-foot trefoil
  • Selfheal
  • Ox-eye daisy
  • Yarrow
  • Salad burnet
  • Wild thyme

For clay soils:

  • Meadow buttercup
  • Ragged robin
  • Devil's-bit scabious
  • Meadowsweet
  • Purple loosestrife
  • Ox-eye daisy
  • Knapweed

How to Make the Meadow Look Intentional

A common complaint about wildflower meadows is that they look untidy. To make the meadow look intentional:

  • Mow paths through the meadow. This shows it is deliberate.
  • Mow a strip around the edge of the meadow, for a defined border.
  • Add a feature like a bench or sculpture.
  • Add a sign like "Wildflower Meadow" or "Wildlife Area".
  • Frame it with a hedge or mown strip.

Common Mistakes

  • Not reducing soil fertility. On rich soil, grass outcompetes the wildflowers. Always reduce fertility first.
  • Not removing cuttings. Cuttings rot down and fertilise the soil, which favours grass over wildflowers.
  • Sowing too much seed. Sow at 4 to 5 grams per square metre. More seed means more competition and fewer flowers.
  • Cutting at the wrong time. Cut in late summer (August to September), after the wildflowers have set seed.
  • Not mowing paths. Without mown paths, the meadow looks abandoned. Mow paths to show it is deliberate.

Frequently Asked Questions

How long does it take for a wildflower meadow to establish?

2 to 3 years. The first year looks sparse, the second year looks promising, and by the third year it should be a dense, flowery meadow.

Will a wildflower meadow attract ticks?

Possibly, but the risk is low in a garden meadow. Check for ticks after walking in long grass, especially in summer.

Can I have a wildflower meadow in shade?

Yes, but the choice of wildflowers is smaller. Choose a woodland mix with wood anemone, primrose, bluebell, and red campion.
