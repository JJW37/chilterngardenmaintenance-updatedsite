How to Deal With Brambles in an Overgrown Garden

Brambles are the most common problem in overgrown UK gardens. They grow fast, tangle into impenetrable thickets, and regrow from any root left in the ground. This guide explains how to deal with brambles in an overgrown garden, from initial clearance to long-term prevention.

The short answer

Quick Answer

To deal with brambles in an overgrown garden: cut the top growth to ground level, dig out every root crown with a mattock, rake up all debris, treat regrowth with glyphosate in late summer, and replant the cleared area. Brambles are the most common overgrowth problem in Chiltern gardens, particularly along woodland edges and in neglected corners.

Why Brambles Take Over

Brambles (Rubus fruticosus) are the most common overgrowth problem in UK gardens, and particularly in the Chilterns where gardens often back onto beech woods and hedgerows. Brambles spread by seed (bird-dropped), by tip-rooting (the long stems root where they touch the ground), and by suckering from the root crown. A single missed root crown can produce a new thicket in a single growing season.

The combination of bird-dropped seed, semi-shade, and the fertile chalky soil of the Chilterns makes bramble thickets particularly aggressive. A neglected corner can become a bramble thicket of 10 square metres in just two years. The thorns make the area impassable, the thicket smothers everything underneath, and the root system is hard to remove.

The good news is that brambles are easier to kill than some other invasive plants. They do not regenerate from root fragments (unlike bindweed), and they do not have deep tap roots (unlike dandelions). With the right approach, brambles can be cleared in a single season.

Step 1: Assess the Situation

Before starting, walk the area and assess what you are dealing with. Note:

  • Size of the bramble thicket (square metres)
  • Density (light, medium, heavy)
  • What is underneath (lawn, border, hard standing)
  • What is around the thicket (fence, hedge, tree, building)
  • Any nesting birds (March to August)

Do not start clearing between March and August if there are nesting birds in the thicket. It is an offence under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 to damage or destroy an active nest.

Step 2: Cut the Top Growth

Use a brush cutter, secateurs or loppers to cut all the top growth to ground level. Wear thick gloves, a long-sleeved top and eye protection. The thorns are vicious and can cause nasty scratches that get infected. Cut the stems into manageable lengths and pile them up for removal.

A 10 square metre bramble thicket will produce 4 to 6 bin bags of cut material. If the brambles are tangled with fences or trees, work carefully to avoid damaging the underlying structure. Pull the cut stems out a handful at a time, rather than yanking whole armfuls which can pull fence panels with them.

Step 3: Dig Out the Root Crowns

This is the most important step and the one most people skip. Brambles regrow from the root crown, the woody swelling where the stems meet the roots. Use a mattock to dig out every crown. Go 15 to 20cm deep to get the whole crown. Any fragment left will regrow.

On chalky soils in the Chilterns, the root crowns sit in relatively free-draining soil and come out cleanly with a mattock. On heavy clay in the Vale of Aylesbury or around Didcot, the soil sticks to the roots and you may need to use a spade as well as a mattock. Either way, the goal is to remove the entire woody crown, not just chop through it.

Step 4: Rake Up All Debris

Rake up all the cut stems, leaves and root fragments. Bramble stems can root where they touch the ground, so do not leave any lying around. Burn the waste if you have a burn permit, take it to the tip, or have it removed by us as part of a clearance (see our clearance service). We take all waste away.

Step 5: Monitor for Regrowth

Check the area every 2 weeks for the first 3 months. Any root fragment you missed will produce a new shoot within 4 to 6 weeks in the growing season. Dig out any regrowth immediately, do not let it get established. A small shoot is easy to dig out, a 6-month-old thicket is not.

Step 6: Treat Stubborn Regrowth

If regrowth persists after 2 or 3 digs, treat with a glyphosate-based weedkiller in late summer (August to September). At this time of year, brambles are sending energy down to the roots for winter, so the weedkiller is carried down to kill the root system. Spray on a dry, calm day when no rain is forecast for 6 hours. Follow the label instructions carefully.

Step 7: Replant the Cleared Area

Once the brambles are gone, you need to replant the area to prevent them coming back. Bare soil is an invitation for weeds and bramble seedlings.

  • Improve the soil with compost if needed.
  • Plant ground cover plants (hardy geraniums, epimedium, bergenia) to suppress weeds.
  • Plant shrubs for structure.
  • Mulch thickly to suppress weed germination.

When to Clear Brambles

Winter (November to February) is the best time to clear brambles. The plants are dormant, the lack of leaves makes the root crowns easier to see, and the ground is softer after winter rain. Avoid clearing in bird nesting season (March to August) if the thicket is being used by nesting birds.

Tools You Need

  • Brush cutter or secateurs and loppers
  • Mattock for digging out root crowns
  • Spade for heavy clay soils
  • Thick gloves, long-sleeved top, and eye protection
  • Wheelbarrow for waste
  • Rake
  • Glyphosate weedkiller and sprayer (for stubborn regrowth only)

Common Mistakes

  • Cutting without digging. Just cutting the top growth leaves the root crown in the ground. The bramble will be back within weeks.
  • Leaving cut stems on the ground. Bramble stems root where they touch soil. Always remove every piece.
  • Using contact weedkiller. Contact weedkillers burn the leaves but do not kill the root. Use glyphosate, or just dig.
  • Not wearing protection. Bramble thorns cause nasty scratches that can get infected. Always wear thick gloves and long sleeves.
  • Clearing in nesting season without checking. Bramble thickets are popular nest sites. Always check carefully before clearing between March and August.
  • Not replanting after clearance. Bare soil is an invitation for brambles and weeds to return. Always replant.

Frequently Asked Questions

Will brambles come back if I just cut them?

Yes. Cutting removes the top growth but leaves the root crown, which will regrow within weeks. You must dig out the root crown to permanently remove brambles.

How do I clear a large bramble thicket?

For thickets over 50 square metres, hire a professional. The volume of waste and the labour involved make DIY impractical. See our clearance service.

How long until I can replant the area?

Immediately after digging out the root crowns. If you have used glyphosate, wait 4 weeks for it to break down before replanting.
